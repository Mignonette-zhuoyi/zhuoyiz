01

The setup — and the surprise

Search incentives had already worked in Japan. The question wasn't whether the mechanic was sound — it was why the US rollout wasn't producing the same result.

In Japan, adding a search task incentive moved core metrics significantly: overall manual search grew +4.2%, with new search users up +9.3% and low-activity users up +14.3%. The mechanic made sense — reward users for building a search habit, and they keep searching even after the incentive ends.

Phase 1 in the US was a controlled test, limited to existing TikTok Shop users. Users who completed 6 daily searches earned a $5 TikTok Shop voucher. Q4 2024 results came back with a paradox.

Despite Japan's validation, US manual search showed no meaningful lift. The original goal of growing search behavior wasn't being achieved.

Phase 2 expanded eligibility from existing TikTok Shop users to all US users (~70M DAU, excluding minors and unsafe accounts). That unlocked a much larger potential audience — and surfaced the harder question: with the right audience now in scope, why was participation still near zero?

How I framed the problem

Before proposing any solution, I pushed to map the full authorization funnel as a diagnostic step. The instinct on the team was to improve the banner design — I argued that we first needed to know where users were actually dropping, and why, before deciding what to build. That framing shaped everything that followed.

02

Diagnosing the funnel

0.2% isn't a design problem. It's a product problem hiding inside a math equation.

I proposed breaking the authorization funnel into two sequential, independent conversion events: exposure and authorization. This was a deliberate framing choice — it separated what was a reach problem from what was a friction problem, and made it possible to prioritize one before the other. The data confirmed that exposure was the bigger lever: fixing conversion without fixing exposure would still leave 86% of users untouched.

At 0.2% of DAU participating, no search metric could move — regardless of how well the voucher was designed. The program needed to fix exposure first, then conversion.
Problem 1 — Exposure

The campaign had a structural ceiling

The T&C pop-up only triggered when a user completed a search and returned to the middle page — catching only already-active searchers. Users who entered search casually, or stayed on the results page, never saw it. The trigger logic guaranteed a low exposure ceiling regardless of campaign quality.

14% T&C pop-up trigger rate
Problem 2 — Conversion

Authorization required a detour

Users who saw the banner couldn't authorize directly. Tapping the CTA routed them to a separate activity detail page — a context switch that introduced hesitation and extra steps. Nearly half dropped before completing. That 49% wasn't disinterest; it was recoverable friction.

03

Product decisions

Three initiatives, each mapped to a specific, measured gap in the funnel. My role was to define the problem framing, align the team on priority order, and contribute to scoping each solution — working closely with design and engineering to validate feasibility.

01 Identify and unlock a new exposure surface — the search results page Exposure +

Optimize the existing banner on the suggestion page. The assumption was that better visual design would improve performance.

The suggestion page wasn't underperforming — it was structurally limited. I argued that optimizing within a surface reaching only 14% of users would never move the aggregate metric. The real opportunity was identifying untapped surfaces, starting with the results page where post-search users were already expressing intent.

New touchpoint for ~25M passive search users

The suggestion page only catches users mid-type. The results page catches users who have already searched — behaviorally different, and far more numerous. Adding the widget here means the campaign finds users at the moment they're most primed to engage, without interrupting the search flow itself.

02 Redefine the trigger logic — expand from return-only to entry + return Exposure +

The trigger was tied to a specific user action — returning from search — which seemed intuitive but created a structural blind spot for first-time entry users.

I questioned why the trigger was return-only when eligibility wasn't conditional on having searched before. Expanding to entry + return was a logic fix, not a design change — and it doubled the exposure opportunity per session at zero engineering cost.

Doubles trigger opportunities per session
Before
  • User opens search (middle page) — no banner shown
  • User types query, browses results
  • User returns to middle page → banner + pop-up appear
After
  • User opens search → banner appears immediately on entry
  • User types query, browses results
  • User returns to middle page → banner + pop-up also appear (retained)

To prevent overexposure: if a user sees the banner X times within 3 days with zero clicks, it steps back for 3 days before reappearing. This keeps the experience from feeling intrusive while ensuring eligible users eventually encounter the campaign.

03 Reduce authorization from 3 steps to 1 — eliminate the detour to the detail page Conversion +

Tapping the banner routed users to a separate activity detail page. Authorization required reading that page then tapping "Join now" there — a multi-step detour that broke search context entirely.

Consolidate everything into the T&C bottom sheet: rewritten copy, a direct "Join now" button, and an inline T&C link. Users authorize without leaving search. "Learn more" remains for those who want the full detail page.

The copy rewrite focused on three things: what you get (voucher value, no minimum spend), what you do (type a search, browse 15 seconds), and what you're agreeing to (T&C link inline). Surfacing the T&C link directly in the bottom sheet removed the last blocker for users who were ready to join but needed a legal reference point.

04

A decision that didn't ship — and why that mattered

The most valuable product judgment I exercised on this project was knowing when to stop pushing.

During scoping, I identified an additional exposure opportunity: adding a campaign banner to the top of e-commerce search results pages, mixed into the results feed. On paper, the logic was sound — e-commerce intent users are exactly the audience for a TikTok Shop voucher, and the results page already had high engagement.

I brought the proposal into a cross-functional review. It was rejected on two grounds:

Reason 1 — Redundancy

Users would already see the widget

The results page widget (Decision 01) would already be visible to these users. Adding a top banner created duplicate exposure without incremental reach — increasing noise without increasing coverage.

Reason 2 — User experience cost

Disrupting a high-intent shopping moment

Inserting a campaign banner into e-commerce search results interrupts users at a moment of active purchase intent. The conversion cost of that disruption likely outweighed the authorization upside.

What I took from this

Good product judgment isn't just knowing what to build — it's recognizing when a seemingly logical idea creates a worse trade-off than the problem it solves. The rejection made the final scope tighter and more defensible. I updated my mental model: exposure maximization has a user experience cost that compounds, and the right question isn't "where else can we show this?" but "where does showing this create genuine value?"

06

What I learned

01

Diagnosis before solution

The team's instinct was to improve the banner. I pushed to map the funnel first. That single framing decision — separating exposure from conversion as two distinct problems — determined what got built and in what order.

02

Product logic changes can outperform design changes

The entry-trigger fix wasn't a design change — it was a logic change. Zero visual redesign, double the exposure opportunities. The highest-leverage product decisions are often invisible on a screen.

03

Know when to kill a good idea

The rejected e-commerce top banner was logically sound but created a worse trade-off than the problem it solved. Learning to recognize when a seemingly right idea has the wrong cost is a core PM skill I developed on this project.

04

Metrics only move when the denominator is right

0.2% authorization rate looked like a conversion problem. It was actually a coverage problem. Getting the denominator right — who is actually eligible, and who is actually being reached — changes everything about how you read product metrics.
