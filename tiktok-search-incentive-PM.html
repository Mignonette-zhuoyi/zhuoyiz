01 · Context

Search incentives had already worked in Japan. The question was why the US rollout wasn't producing the same result.

In Japan, a search task incentive moved core metrics significantly — overall manual search up +4.2%, new search users up +9.3%, low-activity users up +14.3%.

Phase 1 in the US limited participation to existing TikTok Shop users: complete 6 daily searches to earn a $5 voucher. Q4 2024 results returned a paradox.

Working — E-commerce impact

The voucher drove real purchasing behavior among authorized users, exactly as designed.

+8% PayU/DAU · +10% nOrder/DAU

Not moving — Core search growth

Active search growth was ~0. The original goal — growing search behavior — was not being achieved.

~0 active search growth · ~9% auth rate among eligible users

Phase 2 expanded eligibility to all US users (~70M DAU). The right audience was now in scope — so why was participation still near zero?

How I framed the problem

Before proposing any solution, I pushed to map the full authorization funnel first. The team's instinct was to improve the banner — my argument was: understand where users are dropping, and why, before deciding what to build.

02 · Diagnosis

Only ~9% of eligible Phase 1 users authorized — this was a reach problem, not a conversion problem.

I framed the funnel as two independent events: exposure and authorization. Data confirmed exposure was the bigger lever — without fixing it, 86% of users would never see the campaign.

Authorization funnel — target state
Problem 1 — Exposure

The campaign had a structural ceiling

The T&C pop-up only triggered when users returned to the search middle page after searching — missing everyone who entered fresh or stayed on the results page. 14% exposure rate, regardless of campaign quality.

Problem 2 — Conversion

Authorization required a detour

Users who saw the banner couldn't authorize directly — they had to navigate to a separate detail page. That context switch introduced hesitation. 49% dropped — not disinterest, but recoverable friction.

03 · Decisions

Three initiatives, each mapped to a specific gap in the funnel.

Ordered by funnel position: D01–D02 fixed reach. D03 removed authorization friction.

D01

Add a campaign widget to the search results page

Default team assumption: optimize the existing middle-page banner.

The middle page wasn't underperforming — it was structurally limited. Optimizing within a surface reaching 14% of users would never move the aggregate metric. The results page captures users who have already searched — a larger, higher-intent audience. New touchpoint for ~25M passive search users without interrupting the search flow.

D02

Expand trigger logic — entry + return, not return-only

The trigger was tied to returning from search — intuitive, but creating a blind spot for first-entry users.

This was a logic fix, not a design change. Eligibility wasn't conditional on having searched before, so the trigger shouldn't be. Expanding to entry + return doubled exposure opportunities per session at near-zero engineering cost. Frequency cap prevents overexposure: step back for 3 days after X impressions with no clicks.

More exposure per session risks feeling intrusive — the frequency cap is the minimum viable constraint, calibrated not eliminated.

D03

Consolidate authorization into the T&C bottom sheet — 4 steps to 1

Original flow: tap banner → navigate to detail page → read landing page → tap "Join now." Four steps, breaking search context entirely.

Consolidate into the T&C bottom sheet: rewritten copy, inline T&C link, and a direct "Join now" button. Users authorize without leaving search. "Learn more" stays — but is no longer required.

Before — 4 steps

Tap banner → navigate to detail page → read landing page → tap "Join now"

After — 1 step

Tap banner → bottom sheet (copy + T&C link + Join now) → authorized

04 · A decision that didn't ship

Targeting high-value e-commerce users wasn't an upside — it was more interruption.

I identified an additional exposure surface: a campaign banner at the top of e-commerce search results, mixed into the feed. E-commerce intent users are exactly the TikTok Shop voucher audience.

The proposal was rejected in cross-functional review.

Reason 1 — Redundancy

These users already see the widget

The results page widget from D01 already reached this audience. A top banner added duplicate exposure without incremental reach — more noise, no new coverage.

Reason 2 — UX cost

Interrupting a high-intent shopping moment

Inserting a campaign into active purchase intent breaks the moment of highest conversion likelihood. The disruption cost likely outweighed the authorization upside.

What I took from this

Good product judgment isn't only knowing what to build — it's recognizing when a logically sound idea creates a worse trade-off than the problem it solves. Exposure maximization has a user-experience cost that compounds.

05 · Outcome & Reflections

Exposure rate 14% → 67%. Authorization rate 49% → 60%. Shipped in 10 days.

01

Diagnose before you design

Separating exposure from conversion as two distinct, measurable problems determined what got built and in what order.

02

Logic changes can outperform design changes

The entry-trigger fix was a logic change, not a redesign. Zero UI cost, double the exposure opportunities. The highest-leverage decisions are often invisible on screen.

03

Know when to kill a good idea

The rejected e-commerce banner was logically sound but made a worse trade-off. Recognizing that is a core PM skill I developed on this project.

"The most important decision happened before any solution was proposed — the decision to map the funnel first, and resist the instinct to redesign before diagnosing."

— Project retrospective
